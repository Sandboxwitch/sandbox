Assistive Technology Video Transcript

Title Card Reads: What is Assistive Technology? over a pale version of the disability pride flag 

 

  • Speaker: What even is assistive technology? 

 

Slide shows a screenshot of WHO excerpt 

  • Speaker: In their 2022 global report on Assistive Technology, the The World Health Organization wrote that Assistive technology is an umbrella term for assistive products and their related systems and services. Assistive technology enables and promotes the inclusion, participation and engagement of persons with disabilities, ageing populations and people living with chronic conditions in the family, community and all areas of society, including the political, economic and social spheres.

 

Slide shows same text, with sections highlighted and connected to summarized version

  • Speaker: If we break that definition down into simpler terms, AT is any tool or service that supports the participation of people with health concerns



Slide Shows icons of a blanket, a laptop, a hat, headphones, a prosthetic leg, a powerchair, and a cane. 

  • Speaker: That means that AT can be nearly anything! 

 It doesn’t have to be prescribed by a doctor. It doesn’t have to be related to personal mobility. And it doesn’t have to be related to productive occupations.

It just needs to help someone with a health concern engage with an activity 



Slide Shows a table of a smiling person and a gaming controller

  • Speaker: Let’s go through some examples 

Your friend Blue only has one hand.  Blue uses a custom video game controller so they can play online games with you. That video game controller is AT

 

Slide Shows a table of a different smiling person and a fidget cube

  • Speaker: Your roommate Brady has Autism.  He uses a fidget cube to self-regulate when he has to be in crowded places. That fidget cube is AT

 

Slide Shows a table of a different smiling person and a fidget cube

  • Speaker: Kiki is in your history class, but you don’t really know her. You noticed that she always wears these really dark sunglasses. Do Kiki’s sunglasses count as AT?  Well. we can’t really tell. And we shouldn’t ask her 

 

Slide Shows two connected boxes, designed to mimic a common translation app layout

  • Speaker: Questions about AT often feel like a threat. It may feel harmless to ask questions like

“I never see you without the sunglasses, what’s with those?” 

Or 

“Do you really need to wear those all the time?”

But these questions are scary, it feels like you’re saying:

“You’re  different. Tell me your health information so I can judge you”

 

Slide Shows a comic. In the first panel a smiling person loudly questions a frowning person who is using a cane. The second panel shows the person without their cane grimacing in pain while going downstairs. The third panel shows the same person sad and in a house. 

  • Speaker: Questions about AT can be a source of harm. Data shows that people would rather deprive themselves of helpful AT rather than be asked questions about it, even if it costs them their health

or cuts them off from valued occupations

Slide shows three boxes filled with the following text:

  • Speaker: Assistive technology is all around us. Even if we can’t identify it. We don’t need to understand someone to respect them

 

Slide shows three boxes filled with the following text:

  • Speaker: You have the power to make the world more accessible. Accessibility is more than assistive technologies. Accessibility requires a supportive community too

 

End Slide is a thankyou over a pale version of the disability pride flag 

 

  • Speaker: Thankyou

Video ends
